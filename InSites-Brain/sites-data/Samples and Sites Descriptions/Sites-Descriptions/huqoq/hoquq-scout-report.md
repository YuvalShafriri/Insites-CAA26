## Extracted Content for CBSA

### Site Identification & Location
**Source pages: §Table of Contents items 1-4, paragraphs [50]-[52], [109]-[124], [143]-[145]** | Depth: **Rich**

**Horvat Huqoq** (חורבת חוקוק / Huqoq) is identified with biblical Huqoq, mentioned in the tribal territory of Naphtali (Joshua 19:32-34) and as a Levitical city in Asher (1 Chronicles 6:59-60). The site is located on a hilltop west of Road 65, south of the National Water Carrier channel, on the slopes of Mount Habakkuk, overlooking northern Sea of Galilee. The site is approximately 6 km northwest of the Sea of Galilee and Capernaum.

The ancient settlement covered approximately 30 dunams on the hilltop, with the synagogue at its center. The later Arab village Yaquq (يَاقوق) occupied roughly 10 dunams over the central part of the ancient settlement. Below the hilltop lies Ein Huqoq spring at the southern foot of Mount Habakkuk. The adjacent site Sheikh Nashi sits on a hilltop to the east.

**Statutory data**: Site number 3486/0 (Huqoq), 8165/0 (Sheikh Nashi). Official Gazette declaration 3980 (13/07/1995) and 4539 (03/07/1997). Currently designated as agricultural land and grazing (plan G/4208). Municipal jurisdiction: Emek HaYarden Regional Council. The proposed plan 1180314-214 envisions an archaeological park.

### Physical Description
**Source pages: paragraphs [180]-[218], [270]-[288]** | Depth: **Rich**

**The site as landscape**: An archaeological tell on a hilltop with rocky limestone slopes. Two main settlement phases visible: the ancient village Huqoq and the later village Yaquq. The ancient settlement extended ~30 dunams on the upper hill, with the synagogue at center. Surrounding: agricultural terraces on NE slope, rock-cut installations ("Huqoq installations") on western slopes, quarries and tombs defining the settlement boundary, field walls creating agricultural plots, a historic olive grove to the NW.

**Byzantine Synagogue (5th c. CE)**: Galilean-type basilica with three stylobates bearing columns on pedestals. South-facing entrance. Eastern courtyard paved with large dressed stones and surrounded by porticos. Exceptionally rich mosaic floors depicting:
- Samson carrying the Gates of Gaza
- Samson and the foxes (?)
- Crossing of the Red Sea (fish swallowing Pharaoh's soldiers)
- Tower of Babel (workers cutting wood)
- Noah's Ark
- Zodiac wheel (month of Tevet preserved)
- Possibly Maccabean scenes (first extra-biblical narrative in an ancient synagogue if confirmed): elephants with armor, young men with swords around an elder with a scroll, a wounded bull
- Daily life scenes, inscriptions in Hebrew/Aramaic

Architectural elements include fresco-decorated column drums (red and yellow vegetal patterns), stucco, and large dressed limestone blocks.

**Mamluk Synagogue (14th-15th c.)**: Built directly over the Byzantine synagogue, reusing its eastern and northern walls. Basilica plan, 24m × 17.8m. Monolithic pedestals reused from earlier building. Stone benches along three walls. Thick plaster floor with small mosaic fragments (white, geometric and floral patterns). Extremely rare — one of very few identified Mamluk-period synagogues.

**Water System (Ein Huqoq)**: Exceptionally well-preserved Ottoman-Mandate system at the spring:
- Phase A (pre-1875, Ottoman): Rock-cut spring pool with barrel vault, stone-built aqueduct, sabil (drinking fountain) with arch, stone trough for animals
- Phase B (Mandate, to 1946): Northern pool enclosed with basalt walls, southern pool with 10 basalt steps, expanded spring structure
- Phase C (late Mandate, 1946-1948): Concrete additions, iron pipe system, pumping pool, concrete roofing — full modernization
- Post-1948: Abandonment, military use, metal troughs replace stone ones

**Hiding Systems**: Three underground hiding systems:
1. Northern system (under the synagogue) — unexplored
2. Southern system (under the synagogue) — 18m main tunnel leading to shaft with cut steps, blocked in Late Roman when synagogue built above
3. Northern slope system — the largest and most elaborate, 7 chambers connected by narrow winding tunnels. Entered through a plastered mikveh (A) connected to a water cistern (B). Reused Early Bronze Age burial cave. Lamp niches with soot evidence. Attributed to Bar Kokhba revolt preparations (early 2nd c. CE) based on pottery, bronze ring, iron knife. Evidence of 5 phases of use: EB burial → Roman mikveh/cisterns → First Revolt hiding → Bar Kokhba elaborate system → Byzantine and Crusader reuse.

**Agricultural installations**: Dozens of rock-cut "Huqoq installations" — elliptical treading surfaces with collection basins, unique to this site. Debated: either EB wine presses or Roman-period mustard oil presses (connected to Talmudic references). Also: Byzantine wine presses (plastered), olive oil presses (bodedet), game boards (mankala) carved in rock.

**Roads**: Several historical road segments preserved — routes to Tiberias, Safed, Majd al-Kurum, neighboring villages, and the mills of Nahal Zalmon. Main path from spring to village well preserved with flanking walls up to 5 courses high.

**Burial**: Rock-cut trough graves (arcosolia), burial caves from Roman-Byzantine periods (rich assemblages excavated 1956-57), Muslim cemeteries (Ottoman-Mamluk), EB shaft tombs.

**Mikva'ot**: Three ritual baths found — rock-cut with plastered steps, Roman period. A fourth possible mikveh identified in the 2011 salvage excavation. The southern mikveh near fields may indicate agricultural produce destined for Temple purity requirements.

### Contexts
**Source pages: paragraphs [143]-[153], [197]-[201], [246]-[252]** | Depth: **Rich**

**Historical context**: Huqoq sits on a crossroads connecting Tiberias, Safed, Acre-Damascus road, and neighboring villages (Majd al-Kurum, al-Shuna, al-Qudriyya). The spring served travelers and pilgrims for centuries. In the Byzantine period, the settlement was part of the northern Galilee Jewish community alongside Capernaum, Wadi Hamam, Horvat Kur — sharing messianic-apocalyptic traditions reflected in the synagogue mosaics (Samson as messianic prototype, Maccabean martyrdom). Tiberias and Mount Arbel, visible from Huqoq's synagogue, had messianic significance (Bereshit Rabbah 20:7).

**Social context**: Continuous multi-ethnic settlement — Jewish village in Roman-Byzantine periods (Talmudic scholars, mustard industry, purity practices), Muslim village in Ottoman period (agricultural economy — wheat, barley, goats, olives, grapes). The spring was the center of community life, serving Yaquq villagers, their animals, and irrigated plots. After 1948, the spring served Kibbutz Huqoq and Majd al-Kurum herders — becoming part of the kibbutz's collective memory.

**Political context**: The village's destruction in 1948 (conquest, abandonment) and demolition of buildings in 1968 (IDF) are documented. The site is now within the JNF forest framework (TMA'A 1 designation). The current planning process (Plan 1180314-214, led by Shlomo Aharonson Architects for JNF) aims to develop the site as an archaeological park.

**Landscape context**: Mediterranean traditional cultural landscape — terraced hillside with olive groves, field walls, irrigated plots fed by the spring, traditional orchards (orchard vegetation still preserved). The relationship between hilltop settlement, spring below, agricultural terraces, and road network creates a complete "cultural landscape unit." The wider context includes the northern Sea of Galilee Byzantine landscape: synagogues, churches, Tiberias, and the pilgrimage route to Habakkuk's tomb (from the 14th century).

**Religious/ideological context**: The mosaic program reflects apocalyptic-messianic ideology current in late antique Galilean Judaism. Samson narratives in Huqoq and Wadi Hamam synagogues likely carried eschatological messages — hopes for divine redemption from Byzantine Christian rule, drawing on texts like "Sefer Zerubbabel." The possible Maccabean scenes connect to martyrdom traditions usually associated with Christianity but here appropriated by Jewish communities.

### Integrity & Condition
**Source pages: paragraphs [60]-[100], [106]-[107], [299]-[397]** | Depth: **Rich**

**Overall preservation**: Exceptionally good for an archaeological site. The document repeatedly notes remarkable preservation:
- **Synagogue**: Walls partially preserved, mosaic floors in relatively good condition with varying completeness. The eastern stylobate was dismantled in the medieval period (damaging adjacent mosaics). Some mosaics still covered and unexcavated.
- **Water system**: "Preserved in an extraordinary manner" — most Ottoman and Mandate components still functioning at time of excavation. The sabil, pools, pipes, and spring structure survived largely intact through three phases.
- **Hiding systems**: Main northern system well-preserved — tunnels, chambers, lamp niches with soot still visible. Some damage from later quarrying.
- **Cultural landscape**: Field walls, terraces, olive grove, traditional orchard vegetation, historical road network — all substantially preserved.
- **Village Yaquq**: Hilltop ruins remain as rubble (demolished 1968). Cemetery intact.

**Threats/concerns identified in the document**:
- Development pressure from the archaeological park plan itself — need to balance access with preservation
- Physical intervention risk — excavation is inherently destructive
- Mosaic vulnerability — exposure to sun, rain, temperature fluctuations. Need enclosed building with climate control
- Water system recently damaged during rehabilitation works for visitor development
- Subsurface hiding systems vulnerable to unknowing disturbance from surface construction
- Foundation work for roofing structure over synagogue must avoid hidden tunnels beneath

**Conservation philosophy stated**: Minimal intervention, reversible changes only where possible, evidence-based restoration, protection of authenticity of remains and their broader historical, regional, and cultural contexts. Planning should be systemic — seeing all values as creating the complete context.

**Authenticity**: High. Multiple layers of authentic material from Bronze Age through Mandate period. Particular concern for the water system's authenticity after recent rehabilitation works that damaged Ottoman-period elements.

### Comparisons
**Source pages: paragraphs [188]-[191], [253]-[268]** | Depth: **Medium**

**Synagogue comparisons**: The Byzantine synagogue belongs to the "Galilean type" — monumental dressed-stone construction with architectural ornamentation. Within this, it belongs to a newly defined sub-type with colorful mosaic floors (vs. typical stone-paved floors like Capernaum). Other members of this sub-type: Wadi Hamam synagogue (~7 km south) and Horvat Kur (~5 km east).

The mosaic program is compared to Wadi Hamam — both sites share Samson narratives with likely messianic-eschatological meaning. Both synagogues had visual lines to Tiberias and Mount Arbel (sites of messianic significance). The Maccabean scenes, if confirmed, would be the first extra-biblical narrative found in an ancient synagogue — no parallels exist.

The Mamluk-period synagogue is described as extremely rare for its period — "raising important questions about the existence of Jewish settlement in the Galilee in the 14th century CE, a period about which very little is known."

**Hiding systems comparison**: Part of a network of 85 hiding systems found in the Galilee and Judea from the revolt periods. The northern slope system is "one of the largest and most elaborate discovered in the Galilee." It provides key evidence in the scholarly debate about whether the Bar Kokhba revolt extended to the Galilee (most scholars deny this due to absence of destruction layers, but the Huqoq finds provide the first material evidence for reconsideration).

**Water system**: Compared to spring complexes at Eilabun, Mary's Well in Nazareth, and Ein Hod. The completeness of preservation is highlighted as exceptional — few comprehensive excavations of historical spring complexes have been conducted.

**"Huqoq installations"**: Unique to this site — no identical parallels found elsewhere. Compared to similar installations at Hazor region (Amos & Gatzov 2005) and Taanach (Gatzov, Kovalio-Paran & Teper 2009).

### Finds & Material Culture
**Source pages: paragraphs [168]-[172], [254]-[268], [270]-[275], [277]-[280]** | Depth: **Rich**

**Synagogue assemblages**: Fresco-decorated column drums (red and yellow vegetal patterns), stucco fragments, dressed limestone blocks, monolithic pedestals. Mamluk-period ceramics beneath floor provide construction dating.

**Domestic area (Area 2000)**: Glass and pottery sherds, coins, spindle whorl, millstones and grinding stones for flour, rolling stone for roof compaction, olive pits, mustard plant remains (connected to Talmudic reference).

**Burial assemblages (1956-57 excavation)**: Rich grave goods from Roman burial caves (1st-3rd c. CE) — vessels, ossuaries.

**Hiding system finds**: Roman-period pottery (2nd c. CE), bronze decorated ring, unique narrow iron knife — supporting Bar Kokhba dating. Byzantine and Crusader coins indicating later reuse. Lamp niches with soot.

**Water system finds**: Ottoman-period jars (including south Lebanese Rashiyya al-Fukhar pottery, common from 19th c.), glass prayer beads and carnelian (likely Muslim prayer necklaces), glass bracelets, copper rings — fallen during water drawing. Mandate period: lice comb (bone), horseshoe, glazed ceramic vessel base. Post-1948: ammunition casings (latest dated 1968), 50 and 100 pruta coins, "Karna" soda bottles from IDF canteens (1960s-70s).

**"Huqoq installations"**: Dozens of unique rock-cut features — elliptical treading surfaces, collection basins, raised bowls, shallow depressions. Debate: EB wine presses (parallels at Hazor area) or Roman mustard oil presses (ethnographic parallels, Talmudic connection). Also: Byzantine plastered wine presses, olive presses (bodedet type), mankala game boards.

**Quarries**: Numerous rectangular building-stone quarries, Roman-Byzantine dating from pottery in fill. Some quarried through hard nari layer to reach soft limestone below — then carved mikva'ot, cisterns, burial caves, and hiding tunnels at depth.

## Gaps for CBSA

- **Community/stakeholder voices**: No interviews with or references to Yaquq village descendants or their perspective on the site's significance. The Palestinian/Arab narrative of Yaquq's destruction in 1948 is documented factually but not explored as a values question.
- **Intangible heritage**: Limited — pilgrimage tradition to Habakkuk's tomb is noted but not fully developed as a living practice. No information on whether local communities (Majd al-Kurum, Kibbutz Huqoq) have ongoing cultural practices connected to the site or spring.
- **Tourism/visitor impact assessment**: The document recommends an archaeological park but doesn't analyze carrying capacity or visitor impact on the archaeological remains.
- **Comparative analysis depth**: Synagogue compared within Galilean type; hiding systems within Galilee network. But no systematic comparison with similar multi-period hilltop settlements elsewhere in the Galilee or broader region.
- **Condition survey**: While preservation is described qualitatively, there is no systematic condition grading of individual features (e.g., mosaic panel-by-panel condition, structural stability ratings).
- **Publication limitations**: The document notes (paragraph [107]) that presentation of information is limited due to excavators' publication rights — some data may be withheld.
