### 7.1 Carnuntum (Austria)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Archaeological Cultural Landscape of Carnuntum |
| **Location** | Lower Austria, between Petronell-Carnuntum and Bad Deutsch-Altenburg, south of the Danube |
| **Type** | Roman military and civilian settlement landscape |
| **Period** | 1st–5th century CE |
| **Site description** | Over 10 km² of Roman frontier landscape: legionary fortress, canabae, auxiliary fort, civil town with suburbs, two amphitheatres, sanctuaries, burial grounds, water pipelines, temporary camps. Largely preserved underground beneath agricultural land; over 90% unexcavated. Complete geophysical survey 2012–2015. |
| **Significance summary** | The largest archaeological cultural landscape in Austria and the only Roman legionary-fortress complex where the complete urban surroundings survive without modern overbuilding. | **Comparative** | Unique within Austria: all comparable Roman towns overbuilt. Basis: completeness of preservation + non-overbuilt status. Significance "along the middle and upper Danube, even beyond present-day state borders." |
| **Threats** | Landowner appeals against listing (resolved 2022). Agricultural use ongoing but described as non-destructive. |
| **Method** | Austrian Monument Protection Act ("historical, artistic or general cultural significance") + UNESCO OUV criteria. Legal listing 2019/2022. |

### 7.3 Kurese (Estonia)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Archaeological Landscape in Kurese |
| **Location** | Kurese village, Pärnu County, southwestern Estonia, 20 km from coast |
| **Type** | Multi-period settlement and burial landscape — abandoned village |
| **Period** | Bronze Age (1750 BCE) through medieval; continuous from 8th–9th century CE |
| **Site description** | Abandoned village (last inhabitant died 1973; depopulated by Soviet repressions) with 26 registered monuments: stone graves, clearance cairns, cup-marked stones, stronghold ramparts, field banks, settlement layers. Stone fences preserved, village road passable. Connected to Soontagana stronghold by historic bog road. Common buffer zone ~900 hectares. |
| **Significance summary** | A uniquely preserved agricultural landscape where the entire historical structure of a village — settlements, burials, fields, fortifications, routes — survived intact because Soviet deportation emptied it and the land was unsuitable for collective agriculture. "An invaluable resource for the study of Bronze Age and Iron Age society, religion, economy and life" (Minister of Culture order). The accidental preservation makes it possible to study relationships between burial places, fortresses, settlements, and cult sites within one landscape. |
| **Values** | Scientific value (study of long-term settlement-burial-land use relationships); cultural landscape value (multiple time layers); historical value (continuous occupation, Soontagana stronghold connection) |
| **Integrity** | Exceptionally well preserved. Soviet-era abandonment + unsuitability for large-scale agriculture = no modern disturbance. Pasture reclamation revealed additional features. |
| **Comparative** | Buffer zone size and integrated landscape approach "rather exceptional" in Estonian practice. Basis: completeness of preserved landscape structure (not just individual monuments). |
| **Threats** | Forest/field over-management; mine construction; metal detecting (key trigger for legal protection); potential development interest. |
| **Method** | NHB criteria: age, location, scientific value, uniqueness, prominence, continuity of function, written sources, educational value, preservation. 26 sites registered 2018 with common buffer zone. |

| Field | |
|-------|---|

| **Location** | Southernmost Finland, ~100 km southwest of Helsinki, coast of Gulf of Finland |
| **Type** | Medieval stone castle with preserved outer yard |
| **Period** | 1370s–1550s |
| **Site description** | Castle ruin on a rocky hill (originally an island, now mainland due to land uplift). | Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | The Vrouw Maria Wreck |
| **Location** | Northern Baltic Sea, ~90 km off Turku, depth 41 m, Archipelago National Park |
| **Type** | Underwater heritage — 18th-century merchant shipwreck |
| **Period** | Sank 1771 |
| **Site description** | Dutch merchant vessel, ~26 m long, 7 m wide. ~90% of hull preserved, rigging intact, masts rise to 22–24 m depth. Cargo: zinc, dyestuff, sugar, coffee, cloth, mercury, cheese, butter, and art/silver purchased for Catherine the Great. Discovered 1999 after 200+ years. |

| **Comparative** | Only one other snow-rigged ship of comparable preservation (Sjöhästen, Sweden — warship, not merchant). ### 7.7 Tumuli in Hungary

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Protection of Tumuli in Hungary (case: Füzesgyarmat – Bárdos-halom) |
| **Location** | Great Hungarian Plain, Dévaványa plain (specific case). Tumuli found across Hungary. |
| **Type** | Burial mounds / tumuli (kurgans) |
| **Period** | Late Copper Age/Early Bronze Age (3400–2800 BCE, Yamnaja) through medieval. Bárdos-halom area: Neolithic through Late Middle Ages. |
| **Site description** | Thousands of tumuli across Hungary. Bárdos-halom: height 2.7 m, diameters 75 × 55 m, under cultivation but well preserved. Multi-period site around it (Neolithic through Late Middle Ages). 74 kurgans known in the Füzesgyarmat area, 20 with landscape importance. |
| **Significance summary** | Tumuli are not merely graves but complex phenomena: landmarks along ancient routes and borders, sites of folk traditions and later ritual use, often topped by medieval churches. Their significance extends beyond archaeology into landscape identity and nature conservation (ancient steppe vegetation survives on undisturbed mounds). The Bárdos-halom case shows that preliminary documentation in development contexts can preserve significant kurgans through route modification — heritage management as active planning intervention. |
| **Values** | Historical (multiple cultures); landscape (defining features of the Hungarian Plain, landmarks); nature conservation (ancient steppe vegetation, loess grasslands); intangible heritage (legends, folk traditions); community (communal/ceremonial places) |
| **Integrity** | Highly variable. Many ploughed flat or partially removed. Bárdos-halom: "remarkably well preserved despite ploughing." Of 366 mounds at Százhalombatta, many visible only on geophysics/aerial photos. |
| **Comparative** | Internal ranking by four categories: (1) significant landscape-defining kurgans with vegetation/historical values, (2) disturbed, (3) destroyed above ground, (4) completely destroyed. Basis: landscape prominence + vegetation + historical associations. |
| **Threats** | Agricultural plowing; material extraction; infrastructure development (M47 motorway); no enhanced protection for most despite landscape significance. |
| **Method** | Four-category ranking; development risk analysis (phases 1–4). Protection strategy identifies 8 priority site types. No transparent scoring system — case-by-case. |

### 7.9 Hveravellir (Iceland)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Hveravellir |
| **Location** | Mid-highland Iceland, Kjölur plateau, ~600 m altitude, between Langjökull and Hofsjökull glaciers |
| **Type** | Geothermal site / historic travel route / outlaw heritage |
| **Period** | Viking Age through modern |
| **Site description** | Geothermal area (~2.5 km²) on the old Kjölur mountain route. 19 remains in three categories: (1) outlaw remains — Eyvindarkofi (hut in lava bubble), hot spring cooking stones, cave, sheep enclosures, linked to Mountain-Eyvind (1714–1783) and wife Halla; (2) transport cairns along the ancient road, attested since 12th-century Landnámabók; (3) traveler infrastructure — shelter house (sæluhús, possibly Viking Age beneath 1922 structure), cattle enclosure, utility hot springs. |
| **Significance summary** | Nature and culture are inseparable here: geothermal features enabled survival in otherwise uninhabitable highlands. The outlaw remains scored 9/10 — unique nationally, rare, authentic, experientially powerful ("every child in Iceland knows" the Mountain-Eyvind stories), and integral to the highland cultural landscape. The shelter house may contain Viking Age remains beneath the 1922 structure. |
| **Values** | Uniqueness (outlaw hiding places "like no other"); rarity ("nothing comparable in the country"); experience/intangible (folklore, saga connections); authenticity ("not tampered with, except by nature"); cultural landscape; historical connection (Sturlunga saga, Landnámabók) |
| **Integrity** | "Ruins in original place, not tampered with, except by the unkind forces of nature." Remote highland location provides natural protection. |
| **Comparative** | Unique nationally for outlaw remains. Other Eyvind-monuments exist across Iceland but Hveravellir concentration is singular. Basis: uniqueness of type + saga connections + experiential value. |
| **Threats** | Geothermal power plant development (reason for 2007–2008 assessment); tourism; climate change; natural forces. |
| **Method** | 10 value ranges (adapted from Danish SAVE method): Selection, Rarity, Research value, Experience, Authenticity, Cultural landscape, Coherence, Age, Historical connection, Condition. Scored +/0 per monument group. Part of national evaluation of 19 geothermal areas. |

### 7.10 Þjórsárdalur (Iceland)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Þjórsárdalur |
| **Location** | Southern interior highlands, beneath Mt. Hekla |
| **Type** | Cultural landscape — abandoned Viking Age settlement valley |
| **Period** | Viking Age settlement through early 12th century |
| **Site description** | Fork-shaped eroded valley with ~20 farmstead remains. 122 m Háifoss waterfall at entrance. Contains excavated Stöng (famous Viking Age longhouse) and five other excavated sites. First systematically surveyed mid-19th century (first systematic archaeological survey in Iceland). 1939 pan-Nordic excavation established archaeology, tephrochronology, and osteology as professions in Iceland. Protected area 140 km² (2021). |
| **Significance summary** | A settlement landscape "frozen" in the early 12th century — abandoned after Hekla's 1104 eruption and never reinhabited. Farms, routes, fields, meadows, and place names all preserved. Beyond the remains, the valley is the birthplace of Icelandic archaeology (1939 excavations) and represents the evolution from site-based to landscape-based protection — the first time a whole valley (140 km²) was declared protected in Iceland. |
| **Values** | Historical (Viking Age settlement, volcanic event); scientific (birthplace of Icelandic archaeology, tephrochronology); cultural landscape ("búsetulandslag" — settlement landscape as a whole); identity (cradle of Icelandic archaeology); educational |
| **Integrity** | Exceptional. "Frozen in the early 12th century; untouched by later agricultural use or other landscape development." Volcanic ash sealed the remains. |
| **Comparative** | First whole-valley cultural landscape protection in Iceland. Basis: completeness of preserved settlement pattern + volcanic sealing. |
| **Threats** | Tourism (growing numbers); tree planting; hydroelectric power plant (1969, extended); erosion. |
| **Method** | Cultural landscape protection process. Sites protected since 1927; full valley declared 2021. No formal scoring — significance established through evolving consensus. |

|-------|---|

| **Location** | Urban core of Waterford City, southeastern Ireland |
| **Type** | Urban archaeological site — development-led excavation |

| **Threats** | Urban redevelopment (direct trigger); ongoing development pressure in historic core. |
| **Method** | Irish National Monuments Acts (now HAHMP Act 2023). Assessment within planning: referral to National Monuments Service → archaeological assessment → consent → conditions. Criteria: interest, character, integrity, amenity value at local/regional/national/international level. |

### 7.12 Tuba-Zangariyye (Israel)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Tuba-Zangariyye Dolmen Field |
| **Location** | Korazim Plateau, northern Israel, between Sea of Galilee and Hula Valley |
| **Type** | Megalithic burial field |
| **Period** | Intermediate Bronze Age, ~2500–2000 BCE |
| **Site description** | 65+ dolmens and 20+ tumuli over ~0.5 km² on a basalt hill. Part of the larger Korazim field (~500 dolmens), western extension of the southern Syria/upper Jordan megalithic field. Most dolmens small (1.5–3 m chambers); some covering stones up to 15 tons. First documented 1877 by Kitchener ("Hajr ed Dum" — "stone of blood"). |
| **Significance summary** | The Korazim dolmen field is the largest west of the Jordan River and one of the densest megalithic burial fields in the world. Each dolmen attests to developed social organization and technological knowledge. After extensive destruction elsewhere on the plateau, this field is "a rare and almost the last remnant of this cultural landscape." Significance assessment enabled a shift from salvage excavation to value-based management, reversing a development plan that would have destroyed the field. The only case in this collection using CBSA. |
| **Values** | Historical (4,400-year-old burial tradition); scientific (ongoing research since 1877, much unknown); landscape (dolmens define the Korazim Plateau); rarity ("rare and almost the last remnant"); intangible/biblical (possible references in Deuteronomy, Mark); research potential |
| **Integrity** | Tuba-Zangariyye field "unique and well-preserved." Remains "stand almost as built 4,400 years ago." But wider Korazim Plateau severely impacted — dozens of dolmens destroyed by development. |
| **Comparative** | Largest dolmen field west of Jordan; one of densest globally. Part of the vast southern Syria/upper Jordan megalithic field. Basis: density + preservation + role as last surviving concentration in a diminished landscape. |
| **Threats** | Residential development (Ministry of Housing); basalt mining; infrastructure; historical salvage-excavation policy that destroyed numerous dolmens. |
| **Method** | CBSA (Alef, 2021): identify elements/contexts → articulate significance → contextualize → compare (uniqueness, representativeness, integrity, rarity, workmanship) → concise summary. The only case using this method. |

### 7.15 Zamczysko (Poland)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Hillfort 'Zamczysko' ('The Burgstall') |
| **Location** | Kampinos National Park, near Warsaw. Zamczysko Strict Protection Area. |
| **Type** | Medieval ringfort within national park |
| **Period** | 12th–14th century; also WWII partisan heritage |
| **Site description** | Egg-shaped ringfort on wooded dune edge with marshes below. Double ring of ramparts and two moats, clearly visible. ~102 × 78 m; inner rampart ~5 m high. Also carries WWII symbolic value and serves as a model of collaboration between nature conservation and heritage protection. |
| **Values** | Scientific ("time capsule," largely unexcavated); cultural landscape (ramparts, moats, surroundings all protected); symbolic (WWII partisan base); aesthetic/natural ("beautiful green space"); educational (near Warsaw, accessible); collaborative management (nature-heritage cooperation model) |
| **Integrity** | "Original strata and structures, largely preserved in situ." Ramparts and moats clearly visible. Protected by forest cover and strict protection for centuries. Fallen trees not removed (strict protection). |
| **Comparative** | Not compared to other hillforts. Unique combination: archaeological preservation + nature strict protection + proximity to capital. Basis: preservation quality within exceptional protective context. |
| **Threats** | Buffer zone construction; lowered groundwater; waste/fire hazard; "excessive wilderness penetration by people and their pets" (proximity to Warsaw). |
| **Method** | Polish law: archaeological monument definition (underground remains, cultural layers). Registered 1965. NICH evaluation (2009, 2015). Criteria: preservation state, scientific potential, cultural landscape, educational value, aesthetics. |

### 7.16 St Nicholas / Romanesque Cellar, Gdańsk (Poland)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Church of St Nicholas and the Romanesque Cellar |
| **Location** | Historic center of Gdańsk, Dominican Hillock, Main Town |
| **Type** | Urban religious/architectural — discovered Romanesque remains |
| **Period** | Late 12th century (~1190) through post-medieval |
| **Site description** | Foundations of a Romanesque stone church (single nave, two brick towers, apse, 34.5 m long) beneath the 1896 Market Hall, discovered during 2001 renovations. Nearby: Romanesque cellar — only surviving brick late-Romanesque building in Gdańsk — a vaulted refectory of the first Dominican monastery (13th century), 60% preserved with important vault sections. Church burned early 13th century and in 1308 Teutonic attack. Both now publicly accessible: church foundations in Market Hall, cellar as underground archaeological museum (2015). |
| **Significance summary** | The oldest monumental architecture in Gdańsk — physical evidence of the city's pre-Teutonic history, discovered during a commercial renovation. The Romanesque Cellar is the only surviving brick late-Romanesque building in Gdańsk, directly connected to the Dominican Order whose history "is closely interwoven with that of the city." The case demonstrates that discovery-phase advocacy can reverse destructive decisions: the cellar was initially to be filled back in, but community and professional protest saved it. Both sites show that urban heritage can coexist with commercial and public space. |
| **Values** | Historical (oldest architecture in Gdańsk, pre-Teutonic, Dominican Order); architectural (Romanesque foundations, unique vaulted cellar); local uniqueness ("only surviving brick late-Romanesque building"); symbolic (Dominican heritage, city identity); community/social (95% of Poles feel proud of local heritage; 83% see economic/tourist value); educational/aesthetic |
| **Integrity** | Church foundations preserved under Market Hall. Cellar 60% preserved including vault. Conservation and partial reconstruction for museum presentation. Groundwater protection needed. |
| **Comparative** | "Only surviving brick late-Romanesque building in Gdańsk." Part of distinctive Gdańsk urban ensemble — "only a few cities in northern Europe have retained so much pre-18th century urban structure." Basis: local uniqueness + Dominican connection + community value. |
| **Threats** | Commercial renovation (trigger); initial decision to fill in cellar (reversed); groundwater; Market Hall renovation by new owner. |
| **Method** | Criteria in letter to General Monument Preservation Officer: local uniqueness, community relevance, historical/symbolic value, educational/aesthetic value, preservation state. Decision involved VMPO, GMPO intervention, community advocacy. |

### 7.17 Dumbarton Rock (Scotland, UK)

| Field | |
|-------|---|
| **Name** | Dumbarton Rock ('Dumby') |
| **Location** | Dumbarton, Firth of Clyde, 24 km northwest of Glasgow |
| **Type** | Volcanic rock / fortress / rock-climbing heritage |
| **Period** | Late Iron Age through present (military until 1909; WWII; active climbing ongoing) |
| **Site description** | Volcanic plug rising 73 m from flat Clyde estuary at confluence with River Leven. Twin peaks with remains from Iron Age through WWII (anti-aircraft guns). ## Collection Reading

Fifteen sites across 10 countries, spanning from 2500 BCE megalithic burials to living rock-climbing heritage. All include explicit significance discussion, making this a rich collection for cross-cutting analysis — but the diversity of methods and terminology means comparison requires careful reading.

**Landscape is the dominant frame.** Twelve of 15 sites articulate significance in landscape terms — not just the monument but its setting, context, and spatial relationships. Carnuntum's 10 km², Kurese's 900-hectare buffer, Þjórsárdalur's 140 km² protection zone, the tumuli as landmarks across the Hungarian plain. For a heritage manager, this means **site-level management may be insufficient** — many of these sites require landscape-scale strategies, and any prioritization needs to account for the setting, not just the monument.

**The "archive for future research" argument appears in 5 sites** (Carnuntum, Pitschenbergalm, Leithon, Zamczysko, and implicitly the tumuli), framing unexcavated deposits as assets that increase in value as scientific methods improve. This is a powerful preservation argument — but it's articulated explicitly in only a third of the collection, even though it arguably applies to almost all. **If preservation is a priority, this argument could be strengthened across the entire collection.**

**Social and community values are documented in only 4 of 15 sites** (Dumbarton's climbers, Pilis Mountains' volunteers, Gdańsk's public survey, Hungarian tumuli's folk traditions) — yet community connections are evident or implied in most. Hveravellir's universally known outlaw stories, Þjórsárdalur's identity as archaeology's cradle, Raseborg's tourism role — all carry social significance that isn't formally articulated. **This is likely a documentation gap, not an absence of value — and one that matters, because community support often determines whether conservation succeeds in practice.**

**Assessment methods vary so widely that cross-site comparison is impossible without normalization.** Finnish VARK scoring (0–3, total 17), Icelandic +/0 across 10 criteria, Hungarian spreadsheet (5 groups, total 71 points), Austrian qualitative legal assessment, Irish planning-process evaluation, and one CBSA case (Israel). Raseborg's 16/17 and Vrouw Maria's 15/18 look similar but use identical systems; neither can be compared to Pilis's 71 or Hveravellir's 9/10. **If this collection needs to be prioritized, a common framework is necessary.**

**Development pressure is the most common threat** (10 of 15 sites), but the threat profile is diverse: illegal metal detecting affects 3 sites and prompted legal protection in 2; tourism pressure appears in 3 (Hveravellir, Þjórsárdalur, Zamczysko); natural forces affect nearly all. **Recurring threats — especially detecting and tourism — may be more efficiently addressed through collection-wide policies than site-by-site responses.**

**Three sites illustrate how significance assessment changed management outcomes**: Tuba-Zangariyye (assessment reversed a destructive development plan), Waterford (assessment triggered national policy application), Gdańsk (community advocacy reversed a decision to destroy the cellar). These are not just heritage stories — they are **evidence that investing in significance assessment pays off in concrete preservation outcomes.**
