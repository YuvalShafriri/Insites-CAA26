## 7.1. Archaeological Cultural Landscape of Carnuntum
**Country:** Austria | **Type:** Archaeological landscape / Roman settlement complex | **Period:** Roman (1st-5th century CE)
**Significance Level:** International (UNESCO World Heritage component)

### Description
Major Roman frontier settlement including legionary fortress, canabae, civilian town, amphitheatres, and extensive burial grounds. Part of the UNESCO Danube Limes World Heritage. Over 10 km² of archaeological landscape with over 90% still unexcavated and preserved underground.

**Location:** Lower Austria, between Petronell-Carnuntum and Bad Deutsch-Altenburg, Danube Limes

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (Roman frontier / Danube Limes)
- Scientific value (vast unexcavated areas)
- General cultural significance (visible monuments, tourism)
- Landscape value (integrated archaeological landscape)

### Authenticity and Integrity
High integrity due to 90%+ remaining unexcavated. Agricultural use has been largely non-destructive. Some areas affected by historical stone robbing.

### Comparative Context
Other Danube Limes sites; comparable to Vindolanda (UK), Xanten (Germany)

### Assessment Method
Austrian Federal Monuments Authority criteria: historical, artistic, general cultural significance

### Management Context and Threats
Legally listed monument (2022). Archaeological park with museum infrastructure. Ongoing non-invasive prospection (LBI ArchPro).
**Threats:** Development pressure; Agricultural activity; Infrastructure projects

### Key Dates
- 1st century CE - Roman military camp established
- 1885 - First systematic excavations
- 2021 - Danube Limes inscribed as UNESCO WH
- 2022 - Legally listed as monument

### Distinctive Features
One of the best-preserved Roman frontier landscapes in Europe; extensive use of non-invasive prospection technologies

## 7.2. Alpine Landscape at Pitschenbergalm
**Country:** Austria | **Type:** Alpine archaeological landscape / mountain pasture | **Period:** Multi-period (prehistoric through medieval)
**Significance Level:** National / supra-regional

### Description
High-altitude mountain pasture with multi-period archaeological remains including prehistoric pastoral activity, charcoal production, and medieval alpine farming. Discovered through modern survey methods including LiDAR.

**Location:** Upper Austria, Alpine region, mountain pasture at high altitude

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (long-term alpine land use)
- Scientific value (multi-period stratification)
- General cultural significance (traditional alpine pastoral culture)
- Landscape value (intact alpine setting)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Relatively well preserved due to remote alpine location. Minimal modern disturbance.

### Comparative Context
Other alpine archaeological sites in Austria and Switzerland

### Assessment Method
Austrian Federal Monuments Authority: historical significance + general cultural significance evaluation

### Management Context and Threats
Evaluated as part of in-house Federal Monuments Authority project (2020). Expert report on significance produced.
**Threats:** Climate change effects on alpine environments; Changes in pastoral land use; Forestry activity

### Key Dates
- 2020 - Federal Monuments Authority evaluation project
- Prehistoric to medieval - phases of use

### Distinctive Features
Demonstrates significance assessment of landscape-scale alpine archaeology; LiDAR discovery

Variable - some areas well preserved under forest, others affected by modern agriculture

Other Estonian multi-period landscapes

Illustrates tension between heritage protection and forest/land management in Baltic context

## 7.5. Raseborg Castle
**Country:** Finland | **Type:** Castle / fortification | **Period:** Medieval (14th-16th century)
**Significance Level:** National

### Description
Medieval castle ruin with multiple construction phases, reflecting changing political and military conditions in the medieval Baltic region. Includes castle structures, surrounding settlement remains, and landscape context.

**Location:** Raseborg, southern Finland, coastal area

### Values Identified
- Cultural-historic significance (medieval power center)
- Scientific value (architecture, stratification)
- Landscape value (coastal setting)
- Educational value (accessible ruin, tourism)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Castle ruins in variable condition. Scored high (3) on cultural-historic significance.

### Comparative Context
Other medieval Baltic castles

### Assessment Method
Finnish Heritage Agency scoring system: cultural-historic significance, representativeness, rarity, information value, landscape/environmental value. Scale 0-2 or 1-3.

### Management Context and Threats
Finnish Heritage Agency protected site. Scoring system applied with quantified evaluation criteria.
**Threats:** Natural deterioration of ruins; Tourism pressure; Vegetation growth

### Key Dates
- 14th century - Castle constructed
- 16th century - Abandoned

### Distinctive Features
Demonstrates Finnish quantitative scoring approach to significance assessment

## 7.6. The Vrouw Maria Wreck
**Country:** Finland | **Type:** Underwater heritage / shipwreck | **Period:** 18th century (sank 1771)
**Significance Level:** International

### Description
Dutch merchant vessel that sank in 1771 while carrying art collection purchased by Catherine the Great of Russia. Remarkably well-preserved wreck at approximately 41 meters depth in cold Baltic waters. One of the most significant underwater archaeological sites in Finland.

**Location:** Archipelago National Park, southwestern Finland, Baltic Sea

### Values Identified
- Cultural-historic significance (international trade, art history)
- Scientific value (maritime archaeology, ship construction)
- Historical significance (Catherine the Great connection)
- Symbolic value (international cultural relations)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Exceptionally well preserved in cold Baltic conditions. Cargo potentially intact.

### Comparative Context
Other significant Baltic wrecks; Vasa (Sweden)

### Assessment Method
Finnish three-level classification for wrecks; multi-criteria evaluation including cultural-historic significance, representativeness, rarity, information potential

### Management Context and Threats
Protected under Antiquities Act. Located in National Park. Subject of international media attention and political discussion about possible salvage.
**Threats:** Unauthorized diving; Environmental degradation; Salvage attempts

### Key Dates
- 1771 - Ship sank
- 1999 - Wreck discovered
- Ongoing - Protection and monitoring

### Distinctive Features
Demonstrates underwater heritage significance assessment; intersection of art history and maritime archaeology

## 7.7. Protection of Tumuli in Hungary
**Country:** Hungary | **Type:** Burial mounds / tumuli | **Period:** Multi-period (Bronze Age through Migration period)
**Significance Level:** National

### Description
National-scale effort to document and protect burial mounds (tumuli/kurgans) across the Hungarian landscape. Thousands of tumuli from various periods face threats from agriculture and development. Represents a collection-level heritage management challenge.

**Location:** Hungary (various locations, Great Hungarian Plain)

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (multiple cultures and periods)
- Landscape value (defining feature of Hungarian plains)
- Scientific value (unexcavated archaeological potential)
- Symbolic value (connection to steppe cultures)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Highly variable - many reduced by agriculture; some well preserved in pastureland

### Comparative Context
Kurgans in Ukraine, Russia; barrows in UK, Netherlands, Scandinavia

### Assessment Method
Hungarian heritage protection criteria; inventory and mapping

### Management Context and Threats
National legislation (1711 threshold). Ongoing inventory effort. Challenge of protecting thousands of dispersed features.
**Threats:** Agricultural plowing; Infrastructure development; Erosion; Illegal excavation

### Key Dates
- Bronze Age through Migration period - construction
- Ongoing - inventory and protection efforts

### Distinctive Features
Collection-level management challenge; landscape-scale heritage protection; agricultural landscape context

## 7.9. Hveravellir
**Country:** Iceland | **Type:** Geothermal site / historic travel route | **Period:** Medieval to modern (Viking Age onwards)
**Significance Level:** National

### Description
Geothermal area in the Icelandic highlands used as a waystation on historic travel routes across the interior. Contains remains of shelters, sheep gathering structures, and outlaw hideouts. Combines natural heritage (hot springs) with cultural heritage (travel routes, saga connections).

**Location:** Northern Iceland, highland interior between Langjökull and Hofsjökull glaciers

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (medieval travel routes, saga connections)
- Scientific value (archaeological remains in extreme environment)
- Natural heritage value (geothermal features)
- Intangible heritage value (saga traditions, outlaw stories)
- Landscape value (highland wilderness)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Some remains well preserved in highland conditions. Tourism infrastructure has impacted some areas.

### Comparative Context
Other Icelandic highland sites; comparable geothermal heritage sites globally

### Assessment Method
Icelandic scoring system with 10 evaluation areas; Cultural Heritage Agency of Iceland evaluation

### Management Context and Threats
Cultural Heritage Agency of Iceland. Intersection of natural and cultural heritage management. Tourism management challenges.
**Threats:** Tourism pressure (geothermal bathing); Climate change; Infrastructure development; Volcanic activity

### Key Dates
- Viking Age onwards - use as travel route
- ~1930 - archaeological survey
- Modern - tourism development

### Distinctive Features
Nature-culture interface; extreme environment archaeology; saga tradition connections; outlaw heritage

## 7.10. Þjórsárdalur Cultural Landscape
**Country:** Iceland | **Type:** Cultural landscape / abandoned settlement | **Period:** Viking Age / medieval
**Significance Level:** National / International

### Description
Valley landscape with remains of Viking Age and medieval farmsteads abandoned after the 1104 Hekla volcanic eruption. Preserved under volcanic tephra. Includes the reconstructed Stöng farmstead, one of the best-known Viking Age buildings.

**Location:** Southern Iceland, Þjórsá river valley

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (Viking settlement)
- Scientific value (tephra-sealed archaeological deposits)
- Landscape value (volcanic landscape, abandoned settlement pattern)
- Educational value (Stöng reconstruction)
- Intangible value (connection to saga period)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Exceptional preservation under volcanic tephra. Some areas disturbed by river activity and erosion.

### Comparative Context
Pompeii (volcanic preservation); other Icelandic settlement sites

### Assessment Method
Cultural landscape significance assessment by Cultural Heritage Agency of Iceland

### Management Context and Threats
Protected cultural landscape. Stöng reconstruction as public interpretation site.
**Threats:** Erosion; Hydropower development; Tourism; River course changes

### Key Dates
- 874+ - Viking settlement
- 1104 - Hekla eruption, abandonment
- 1939 - Excavation of Stöng

### Distinctive Features
Volcanic preservation creating "frozen in time" moment; connection between natural disaster and cultural heritage

## 7.11. Waterford City Viking Triangle

**Significance Level:** National (monuments of national significance)

### Description
Historic urban core of Waterford, Ireland's oldest city, with continuous occupation from Viking foundation. Archaeological excavations revealed extensive Viking and medieval remains. Now a heritage quarter integrating archaeology into urban development and tourism.

**Location:** Waterford city center, southeastern Ireland

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (oldest Irish city, Viking foundation)
- Archaeological value (extensive stratified deposits)
- Urban heritage value (living historic city)
- Social value (community identity, tourism)
- Educational value (museum and interpretation)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Deeply stratified urban deposits. Some areas excavated; extensive deposits remain in situ beneath modern city.

### Comparative Context
Dublin Viking remains; York (Jorvik); Bergen (Bryggen)

### Assessment Method
Irish National Monuments legislation; archaeological significance criteria within development control

### Management Context and Threats
National Monuments Act protection. Integration of heritage into urban regeneration through Viking Triangle Heritage Quarter.
**Threats:** Urban development pressure; Infrastructure modernization; Flood risk

### Key Dates
- 914 - Viking foundation
- Medieval period - major town development
- 1980s-2000s - Major excavations
- Modern - Heritage Quarter development

### Distinctive Features
Successful integration of archaeology into urban regeneration; development-led archaeology in living city

## 7.12. Tuba-Zangariyye Dolmen Field
**Country:** Israel | **Type:** Megalithic burial field | **Period:** Intermediate Bronze Age (~2500-2000 BCE)
**Significance Level:** National / Regional

### Description
Dense concentration of 65+ dolmens and 20+ tumuli on the basalt Korazim Plateau. Part of a larger regional megalithic landscape. Threatened by residential development. Significance assessment led to revised development plan incorporating dolmen preservation.

**Location:** Korazim Plateau, northern Israel, between Sea of Galilee and Hula Valley

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (4,500-year-old burial tradition)
- Scientific value (megalithic construction, unexcavated potential)
- Landscape value (megalithic cultural landscape)
- Rarity (one of last remaining intact dolmen concentrations)
- Biblical/intangible value (possible references in Deuteronomy and Mark)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Well-preserved concentration, though dozens of dolmens destroyed in wider Korazim Plateau. Some covering stones weigh up to 15 tons.

### Comparative Context
Korazim Plateau wider dolmen field; Golan Heights megalithic sites; southern Syria megalithic field

### Assessment Method
Context-Based Significance Assessment (CBSA) model (Alef, 2021). Five-step process: identify elements/contexts → articulate significance → contextualize → compare → summarize.

### Management Context and Threats
Israel Antiquities Authority. Shift from salvage-excavation approach to preservation-in-situ. CBSA used to argue for plan revision.
**Threats:** Residential development (Ministry of Housing); Infrastructure construction; Basalt mining; Historical salvage-excavation policy

### Key Dates
- ~2500-2000 BCE - Dolmen construction
- 1877 - Kitchener survey (first report)
- 2015 - Development threat
- 2019 - Revised survey with significance assessment

### Distinctive Features
Demonstrates CBSA methodology in practice; shift from salvage to preservation; biblical landscape connections

## 7.13. Leithon
**Country:** The Netherlands | **Type:** Early medieval settlement | **Period:** Merovingian and Carolingian (6th-9th century)
**Significance Level:** National (scheduled monument)

### Description
Remains of an early medieval settlement along the former Oude Rijn river course. Scheduled as national archaeological monument. Site includes settlement remains, wooden/stone constructions, human remains, and animal bones. Previously used as tennis courts.

**Location:** Leiderdorp, near The Hague and Leiden, western Netherlands

### Values Identified
- Scientific value (early medieval settlement patterns)
- Historical significance (Merovingian/Carolingian occupation)
- Rarity (well-preserved early medieval site in urban context)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Archaeological deposits preserved beneath modern surface (tennis courts). Depth provides some protection.

### Comparative Context
Other Rhine delta early medieval settlements; Dorestad

### Assessment Method
Dutch rating system: intrinsic (scientific) value, physical quality, absolute quality. National scheduling criteria.

### Management Context and Threats
Scheduled national archaeological monument. Cultural Heritage Agency of the Netherlands oversight.
**Threats:** Urban development (densely populated area); Infrastructure works; Water table changes

### Key Dates
- 6th-9th century - Settlement active
- 1950s - First excavation
- 1980s - Human/animal remains found
- Modern - Tennis courts on site

### Distinctive Features
Demonstrates Dutch intrinsic-value-based assessment system; preservation under modern urban surface

Policy-level discussion of prehistoric barrows as repositories of future scientific knowledge. Argues for preservation as archives that will yield information through future technologies not yet available.

Variable. Many barrows leveled by agriculture; surviving examples increasingly rare.

### Comparative Context
UK round barrows; Scandinavian burial mounds; Hungarian tumuli (case 7.7)

### Assessment Method
Future research value assessment; Dutch heritage management framework

### Management Context and Threats
Cultural Heritage Agency of the Netherlands. Policy argument for preservation over excavation.

### Key Dates
- Neolithic-Bronze Age - Construction
- Ongoing - Policy development

### Distinctive Features

## 7.16. Church of St Nicholas and the Romanesque Cellar, Gdańsk
**Country:** Poland | **Type:** Urban religious / architectural site with buried remains | **Period:** Medieval (12th-15th century)
**Significance Level:** National

### Description
Medieval church with Romanesque-period cellar discovered beneath. Complex urban archaeological site where above-ground medieval architecture overlies earlier buried remains. Demonstrates challenges of managing multi-period urban heritage.

**Location:** Gdańsk old town, northern Poland

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (Gdańsk medieval urban development)
- Architectural value (Romanesque cellar, Gothic church)
- Scientific value (urban stratification)
- Symbolic value (Gdańsk identity, post-war reconstruction context)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Romanesque cellar well preserved beneath later construction. Urban context both protects and threatens.

### Comparative Context
Other Hanseatic city archaeological sites; York, Lübeck

### Assessment Method
Polish heritage protection criteria; architectural and archaeological combined assessment

### Management Context and Threats
Listed monument. Complex interplay between above-ground and below-ground heritage management.
**Threats:** Urban development; Vibration from traffic; Water table changes; Deterioration of below-ground remains

### Key Dates
- 12th century - Romanesque phase
- 13th-15th century - Gothic church
- WWII - Destruction and reconstruction of Gdańsk
- Modern - Discovery of Romanesque cellar

### Distinctive Features
Above-ground + below-ground heritage interplay; post-war urban reconstruction context

## 7.17. Dumbarton Rock
**Country:** Scotland, UK | **Type:** Volcanic rock / fortress / cultural landmark | **Period:** Iron Age to modern
**Significance Level:** National (Scheduled Ancient Monument + other designations)

### Description
Dramatic volcanic rock formation that served as capital of the ancient kingdom of Strathclyde (Alt Clut). Multiple heritage designations. Unique case where community participation (rock climbers) led to expanding the official Statement of Significance to include climbing heritage.

**Location:** Dumbarton, west-central Scotland, confluence of River Leven and Clyde

### Values Identified
- Historical significance (Alt Clut, medieval fortress)
- Natural heritage value (volcanic geology)
- Social value (rock climbing community)
- Intangible heritage (community connections)
- Landscape/scenic value (dramatic volcanic rock on river)

### Authenticity and Integrity
Rock itself permanent; built structures in various states. Archaeological deposits partially investigated.

### Comparative Context
Edinburgh Castle Rock; Stirling Castle; other Scottish fortress-rocks

### Assessment Method
Historic Environment Scotland Statement of Significance format: chronological summary, thematic discussions, social overview, assessment of significance with period-based rankings.

### Management Context and Threats
Historic Environment Scotland site in care. Multiple legal designations. Community co-production of Statement of Significance.
**Threats:** Natural weathering; Climate change; Visitor pressure; Military heritage deterioration

### Key Dates
- Iron Age - First fortification
- 5th-11th century - Kingdom of Strathclyde capital
- Medieval-modern - Military fortress
- Modern - Community climbing significance recognized

### Distinctive Features
Community co-production of significance; rock climbing as heritage value; nature-culture integration; first community-contributed Statement of Significance
